# Claude Pressure-Test Output: Sitemap vs. Claim + Action

## Hero Page

**Serves the Head of AI specifically, or generic?**
"I ship domain-specific AI products from API to UI" is close, but it's still a claim *about you*, not a signal *to them*. A Head of AI skims dozens of portfolios. They're pattern-matching for "does this person understand my actual problem" in under 5 seconds. Right now your hero states your positioning, not their pain. It's generic in the sense that it could headline any AI builder's site.

**Proves the claim, or decoration?**
Pure decoration. A hero page never proves anything — it's a promise. That's fine, that's its job. Just don't mistake it for proof.

**Leads to the action, or dead end?**
Depends entirely on whether there's a CTA. If it's just a headline and a scroll-cue, it's a soft dead end — the visitor has to *want* to keep digging. You need an explicit next-step, not passive momentum.

**Cut/change/add:**
- Change the copy from a *claim* to a *diagnosis*: something that names the exact translation problem a Head of AI has — "raw API → conversational product" is the gap you close. State the gap, not just your skill.
- Add a one-line proof teaser under the headline (e.g. "Case study: turned a coffee-data API into a conversational expert in [X days]") so skeptics get instant grounding.
- Add a real CTA button, not just "learn more."

## Work/Case Study Page

**Serves the Head of AI specifically?**
This is your best shot at specificity — *if* it's written for their decision criteria, not for a general audience or recruiters. Brutal question: does it show the *architecture decisions* a Head of AI cares about (API integration, latency, hallucination control, conversation design, evaluation) — or does it read like a general "cool project I built" blog post? Most portfolio case studies fail here — they show *what* was built, not *how you think* about the hard tradeoffs.

**Proves the claim, or decoration?**
This is the only page that CAN prove the claim. Everything else is framing around this page. If this page is weak, the whole site is weak, no matter how good the hero copy is.

**Leads to the action, or dead end?**
This is usually where portfolios die. People read the case study, go "cool," and leave. There's no page-level CTA. This page needs its own bridge to Contact — not just relying on nav.

**Cut/change/add:**
- Cut anything that's about the coffee domain for its own sake. The Head of AI doesn't care about coffee. They care about the *pattern*: raw domain API → structured problem → conversational UX → shipped product. Make coffee the vehicle, not the subject.
- Add explicit "if you're building X, this is why it matters to you" framing at the end of the case study.
- Add a mid-page or end-page CTA: "Facing a similar API-to-UI problem? Let's talk." Not just hero/contact-page CTA.

## About Page

**Serves the Head of AI specifically?**
Almost always the weakest page on any portfolio, and it's usually generic by default — "I'm a builder who loves AI and coffee ☕" energy. Ask: does this page tell a Head of AI anything that changes their decision to reach out? If not, it's dead weight.

**Proves the claim, or decoration?**
Decoration, unless you use it to show *pattern of behavior* — e.g., you've done this domain-API-to-product translation more than once, or you have relevant experience (production systems, ambiguous specs, cross-functional shipping) that de-risks hiring/contracting you.

**Leads to the action, or dead end?**
Near-total dead end in most portfolios. People land here out of curiosity, not intent, and bounce.

**Cut/change/add:**
- Cut generic bio filler (hobbies, general passion statements).
- Cut it entirely as a standalone page, or radically shrink it to a "credibility strip" (3 bullet points: relevant experience, notable outcomes, why you're not a one-off — you can do this again for someone else's domain).
- Consider: does this need to be a full page, or a sidebar/footer on the case study? A 4th page is a 4th chance for them to lose momentum before reaching Contact.

## Contact Page

**Serves the Head of AI specifically?**
Should be the most specific page on the site, and usually is by default — but check: does the CTA copy speak to *their* need ("Let's turn your domain API into a live product") or is it generic ("Get in touch")?

**Proves the claim, or decoration?**
Not its job. Its job is conversion, not proof.

**Leads to the action, or dead end?**
This IS the action page. The real question: how many pages/clicks does someone have to survive to get here? Every page before this is a potential exit ramp.

**Cut/change/add:**
- Make the CTA restate the value prop in their language, not yours: "Building a domain-specific AI product? Let's talk about turning your API into something people actually use."
- Lower friction — don't make them fill out a long form to "reach out." Email/Calendly link, one click.

## The brutal structural problem

You have **4 pages** and only **1 of them proves anything** (the case study). The other three are framing, bio, and conversion — none of which are evidence. That's a normal portfolio shape, but it means the case study is doing 100% of the persuasive work, and right now your plan doesn't guarantee it's optimized for *this specific reader's decision criteria*.

## The ONE thing to change before you build

**Don't treat this as a 4-page site with a nav bar. Treat it as one persuasive argument with four stops — and rewrite every page's copy to answer "why should a Head of AI who needs API→conversational-UI care about *this specific line*?"**

Concretely: before you touch layout or design, rewrite your case study's opening paragraph and your hero's headline so both explicitly name the Head of AI's actual problem (translating a raw domain API into a live conversational product) — not your skill, not the coffee angle. If a Head of AI can't see themselves in the first two sentences of your hero AND the first two sentences of your case study, they won't read far enough to reach Contact, no matter how good the sitemap is.

Everything else — About, page count, nav structure — is secondary. Fix the *specificity of the language* first, or you'll polish a beautiful site that still reads as generic.
